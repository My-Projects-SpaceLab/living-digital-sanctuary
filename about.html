The problem

Every screen you open
was built to take something from you.

Notifications timed to interrupt your peak focus. Infinite scroll that removes the natural stopping point. Badges that trigger anxiety. Autoplay that removes your choice to stop.

Your eyeball-time is money. So every screen is engineered to grab you, hold you, drain you.

The result is what researchers call Directed Attention Fatigue — when your brain's focusing system gets depleted. You feel irritable for no reason. You reach for your phone even though you don't want to. You can't concentrate and you can't figure out why.

"Every existing solution puts the burden back on you. But if you're already depleted — you have no capacity left to use it."

This is something different. It doesn't ask you to do anything.

How it works

It reads you. Then it
responds.

The Sanctuary watches how you interact — not what you type, but how. Your speed. Your pauses. The gaps between thoughts. From that, it figures out where you are and shifts the visual field around you.

Flow — you're in good shape

Cool blue-teal field, fluid motion, full presence. It matches your energy and keeps you with it.

Drift — slowing down

Colors warm, motion softens, the card fades back. The space quietly wraps around you.

Restore — you've gone quiet

Warm amber, barely-there interface, just the breathing field. No demand. Your attention finally rests.

Transitions are slow enough that you don't notice them happening. You just notice, later, that you feel different.

A real scenario — 3pm, you've been working since 8

You open the Sanctuary. You start writing. Anything — what you're feeling, what's been sitting heavy. The words don't need to be good.

Over the next few minutes your typing slows. Imperceptibly, the light warms. The particles slow their drift. A breathing pulse threads through the background.

You didn't ask for it. The space just changed around you. Ten minutes later you close it. Your shoulders are lower. The next two hours are your most focused of the day.

The science

130 years of research
pointed here.

This isn't a wellness trend. Every mechanism is built on peer-reviewed research.

  • Attention Restoration Theory (Kaplan & Kaplan, 1989) — Slow, organic, non-demanding motion is the specific ingredient that allows the directed attention system to recover. The same reason you feel better after sitting near water.
  • Stress Recovery Theory (Ulrich, 1984) — Biophilic visual patterns and warm natural color ranges trigger measurable physiological stress reduction within minutes.
  • Biocybernetic interfaces — Typing speed is a reliable passive signal of cognitive state. Adapting visual complexity to it produces real focus improvements and reduced task errors.
  • Calm Technology (Weiser & Brown, Xerox PARC, 1995) — Technology should live in the periphery of attention, shifting to center only when needed, then quietly returning.

"The brain cannot force itself to rest — but the right environment can make it happen automatically."
